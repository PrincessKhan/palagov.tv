Introduction: Motivations

Crossing the South Pacific Ocean on a small vessel is by no means an easy feat, though nothing worth having is easily obtainable.

The smaller your boat, the higher the chances of capsizing, and the longer you spend in an upside down position – the higher your chances of drowning and depriving your body of the oxygen that it needs to keep your bodily functions running.

This is the reason why record-breaking ocean vessels tend to be equipped with a water-tight 'capsule' which the sailor can climb into in the event of a storm, and a subsequent capsize.

Mind you – the reason we're talking about small vessels, as opposed to venturing out into the open sea with a 40ft yacht that is actually fit for this purpose, is because big boats are far too expensive, and thus – completely out of the reach for the likes of people like myself, and others living close to the poverty line.

If you had to pick between crossing the ocean on a big boat vs. crossing the ocean on a small boat, you should obviously go with the bigger boat.

But then again – this is not a choice that most of us aspiring sailors actually have, which means that we're going to have to risk our lives and put our intellect to good use if we are to attain the things we desire within our lifetimes, particularly now that we live in an age where the average person will quite literally: Own Nothing, and Be Happy.

In the words of the World Economic Forum: You will own Nothing, You will take your Anti-Depressants, You will clock in your 9-5 hours in order to sustain your Flesh, and keep the engines of society running – you will have your pizza delivered to you by drone – and then you will go back to your bedroom to watch Netflix, masturbate, smoke weed, and play video games.

So quite literally: living out your adventures through pixels on a screen, as opposed to experiencing those adventures first-hand, with the naked eye.

There is a reason why the type of content you consume on T.V. tends to be so violent and packed with chaos and destruction.

Because deep down, this is what the Human soul actually craves, in spite of our dishonest attempt at crafting a sterile, clinical world of Health and Safety where nothing exciting happens, and everything is stagnant and predictable. (Except on the screen, where the monsters can't hurt you).

Don't say I didn't warn you.

Most of the uninhabited islands of the Pacific Ocean are what they call 'wildlife reserves' and sanctuaries for birds, and sea turtles. Despite the fact that those same birds and sea turtles are choking on plastic bottles as we speak, nevermind the micro-plastics.

Metric tonnes of garbage wash up on just about every beach around the world – particularly the beaches of Henderson Island a la Pitcairn – where the hermit crabs are now using jars and bottle caps instead of seashells, for a place to call home.

There sure seems to be a lot of Free Real Estate waiting for us beyond the horizon, at least in my opinion – as long as you can arrive there safely, and as long as you can fight off, or outsmart anyone who decides to come after you.

Now back to the topic of space suits – the lack of oxygen is by no means the only chemical imbalance that can kill you on your way to the Island.

So-called Full Face Snorkel Masks have been known to cause multiple deaths from 'unexplained causes' – which they later on found out was attributed to the build up of carbon dioxide that some of the cheaper, poorly-designed variations of these devices can potentially cause, especially if one is doing intensive swimming, and exercise.

So they essentially provide a false sense of security to the diver, when they would have otherwise run out of breath and swum to the surface of the water, instead of dying of carbon dioxide poisoning.

There's two ways you could potentially fix this problem. Either:

  1. Increase the volume of air trapped inside of your mask,
    so as to extend the period breathability when underwater,
    at least long enough to survive drowning when
    capsized by storms, and waves.
  2. Integrate a wider opening and one-way valve at the top of the helmet
    that automatically shuts off through an electronic mechanism,
    triggered by an accelerometer.
  3. Both.

So you would practically have a type of 'space suit' that would be water-tight in just about every part of your body, except the very top of your head, where the accelerometer-controlled flap will prevent your helmet from flooding, should you capsize your inflatable boat.

It must also be highly breathable and provide plenty of ventilation, perhaps with the help of some 12 volt fans hooked up to a rechargeable, solar-powered battery system.

The flap should ideally also have an emergency release, if in case there's an electrical malfunction that stops it from shutting automatically, or worse yet, closing itself off while you're sleeping.

You could actually build such a suit for less than $500 in total, considering how cheap it is to source not just the electronics, but also the fabric, materials, fibre-glass and acrylic dome for your visor, and helmet.

So rather than building an unsinkable boat, you [become] the unsinkable boat – which gives you far more freedom and flexibility in rough weather compared to just toughing it out in a floating barrel, or something like that.

It'll also allow you to survive the whole trip on an inflatable boat of your choosing – provided you have enough food and fresh water to last you through a 1,000km-long voyage, which at 3 knots would take around 7-10 days.

The inflatable canoe should ideally come equipped with an outrigger for maximum stability, a small sail, a rudder hooked up to a DIY auto-pilot system, a 'bicycle-valve' adapter to keep the boat inflated at maximum pressure, as well as a 2-stroke, 2-horse-power outboard engine – because the faster you can go, the safer you will be from sharks, and storms.

Much like the marvels of engineering put together by the Polynesians, who were the first to design such a boat, so as to enable them to conquer the South Pacific Ocean with relatively simple and primitive technology, albeit without the electronics and space suits that White Men like ourselves love to use.
